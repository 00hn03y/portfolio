Subtheme: Speculation

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Who is driving up the home prices in Boston? How?
    • My intuition about Boston is that corporate landlords are increasingly acquiring residential properties, shifting ownership away from individual homeowners and profiting off of rent increases. Having read the ProPublica article, which highlights the role of private equity firms in rent increase, I became curious about the role of real estate investors in driving the speculative housing market. Given Boston’s rising home prices, I want to explore the characteristics of property buyers. Are they banks, firms, LLCs, government entities, etc?
  2. What are the characteristics of the neighborhoods with the most frequent/volatile flipping practices?
    • Prior studies have found that house flipping is often concentrated in neighborhoods with lower initial home values and a high proportion of renters (Immergluck, 2018). I want to investigate whether these trends hold true in Boston and what demographic or economic factors might be influencing them.
  3. Is Black population vulnerable to speculative residential investment?
    • Historically, redlined Black neighborhoods have been targeted by predatory real estate practices, leading to cycles of displacement. Given that Boston remains one of the most segregated cities in the US, I want to explore whether Black communities are disproportionately affected by speculative investment.
Immergluck, D. (2018). "Old wine in private equity bottles? The resurgence of contract-for-deed home sales in US urban neighborhoods." International Journal of Urban and Regional Research, 42(4), 651-665.

Discoveries & Insights

Phase 1: Data Overview

Summary

  • Corporate ownership is rising across all Boston neighborhoods, with certain areas experiencing sharp increases. East Boston and South Boston Waterfront have seen the steepest increases in corporate ownership, suggesting a shift toward investor-driven housing markets. Corporate ownership and vacancy rates are also positively correlated, indicating speculative property holding. Figure 3 suggests that corporate investors may be acquiring properties and leaving them vacant, reducing available housing supply and driving up prices.
  • Furthermore, corporate buyers, especially LLCs, consistently pay higher prices than individual buyers, contributing to rising home values. The home price trends by buyer type visualization (Figure 5) supports concerns that institutional investors are outbidding local homebuyers, making homeownership less accessible.
  • House flipping is not widespread, but certain neighborhoods have notable activity. The flipping trends chart reveals that only seven neighborhoods have measurable flipping activity, with flip rates between 3-4%. This suggests flipping is localized rather than citywide, but its effects on price dynamics should be explored further. Neighborhoods with high flip rates also tend to have high corporate ownership and vacancy rates.
  • >Black-majority neighborhoods, particularly Roxbury and Dorchester, have high corporate ownership rates, raising concerns about displacement. The Black Population vs. Corporate Ownership chart (Figure 7) shows that while flipping isn’t widespread, corporate acquisition of housing in Black neighborhoods could lead to future displacement pressures.
